01

Otis, Enterprise Mobility Platform Workflow Research

Identifying where and why an enterprise field service platform was failing across three user roles before redesign

OtisEnterpriseContextual InquiryB2B
+
Client
Otis Elevator Co.
Domain
Enterprise / B2B
Methods
Heuristic Evaluation, Contextual Inquiry, Moderated UT, Workflow Analysis
My role
Research lead, moderation, synthesis, stakeholder reporting
01 Research question
Where, how, and why is this enterprise field service platform failing its users, and what does the team need to know before investing in a redesign?
02 Why this method

I started with a heuristic evaluation before recruiting any participants. The platform had years of accumulated design debt and I needed a severity map before writing a discussion guide, otherwise sessions risk confirming what we already suspect rather than finding what we don't know. Once I had that map, I chose contextual inquiry over standard interviews because this was a workflow problem. Field technicians operate in low light with gloves on, moving between floors. Watching someone use a platform in a conference room doesn't surface that. Finally, moderated usability testing validated specific task flows and gave us completion rates to quantify friction across all three user roles.

03 How I worked
01
Heuristic audit

Severity-rated 30+ issues across Nielsen heuristics before any participant contact

02
Contextual inquiry

Observed technicians, coordinators, and ops managers in their actual work environments

03
Moderated UT

Task-based sessions across 3 roles capturing completion rates, errors, and think-aloud

04
Affinity mapping

Clustered observations by role and workflow stage to separate shared vs. role-specific failures

05
Prioritised report

Mapped findings to business impact and implementation effort for stakeholder decision-making

🔎

How I synthesised: I mapped every observation to a workflow stage and user role in FigJam, then looked for where friction overlapped across roles at the same stage. Workarounds were the primary signal, every time a user reached for their phone, a spreadsheet, or a colleague instead of the platform, I treated that as a design failure, not user error. That reframe shifted how the team prioritised.

04 What participants said

I check the app, it tells me the job is pending. I call the coordinator, she says it's already been assigned. The app just doesn't know that yet. So I always call first now before I even open it.

Field Technician

I have a spreadsheet I've been keeping for two years. It has everything the system should have. My manager knows about it. Everyone uses it.

Service Coordinator
05 Key findings and outcome
Workaround culture was endemic and team-wide

Users had built parallel systems, spreadsheets, messaging groups, because the platform couldn't surface the right information at the right time. These weren't individual habits; they were documented, collective adaptations that the team had normalised.

Cross-role handoffs were invisible in the system

When a task moved between technician and coordinator, neither party had reliable status visibility, leading to duplicate calls, missed escalations, and eroded trust in whether the platform was ever accurate.

IA was built around the org chart, not the task flow

Navigation hierarchies mirrored internal structure. Users had to know how the company was organised to find what they needed, high cognitive load on every routine interaction.

📍
What changed

The team had a severity-ranked backlog grounded in observed behaviour, not assumptions. The cross-role handoff gap, a problem the team had not formally identified before research, became the primary design problem for the next sprint.

Meta, MaxTAS Panel Research & Usability Testing

Evaluating whether a redesigned panel in Meta Business Inbox actually works for SMB users managing customer conversations

MetaUsability TestingConversational CommerceSMB
+
Client
Meta
Domain
Conversational Commerce
Methods
Moderated UT, Think-Aloud, Prototype Testing, Daily Debriefs
My role
Full research ownership, screener design, moderation, topline report
01 Research question
Does the redesigned MaX/TAS panel solve the usability problems it was built to fix, or does it introduce new ones for SMB users managing orders in chat?
02 Why this method

This was an evaluative study on a specific redesign, so moderated prototype testing with a think-aloud protocol was the right fit, I needed to capture not just whether users could complete tasks, but where they hesitated, what they misread, and what they expected that wasn't there. I recruited from active SMB advertisers rather than general users because the panel is deeply embedded in real business workflows. General consumers would give us surface reactions; business owners gave us operational friction. I ran daily debriefs after each session, a deliberate choice that let me iterate the discussion guide in real time rather than surface the same gaps seven times.

03 How I worked
01
Screener design

Built criteria targeting active SMB advertisers across clothing, custom goods, and retail

02
Discussion guide

Structured guide covering order management, payment tracking, label customisation, and order creation

03
7 moderated sessions

1:1 remote sessions with think-aloud protocol across 3 weeks

04
Daily debriefs

Post-session debriefs captured emerging patterns and allowed real-time guide iteration

05
Topline report

Synthesised findings into prioritised recommendations across 4 workflow areas

🔎

How I synthesised: I organised observations by task and panel section, then flagged moments where multiple participants hit friction at the same point, those were the highest-confidence findings. I separated "the design wasn't clear" problems from "the business workflow isn't supported" problems, because they require different responses from the product team.

⚠️

Constraint worth noting: With only 7 participants, any finding that appeared in 2 or fewer sessions had to be flagged as directional rather than conclusive. The payment integration gap appeared in nearly every session, which gave it strong confidence. The multi-order ID issue was raised by a subset of participants in specific business types, so we framed it as a clear need for custom-product sellers rather than a universal gap. That distinction mattered for how the product team prioritised it.

04 What participants said

If the same customer is buying different products, the IDs should be different. Right now I can't tell which order is which.

Retail business owner

Integration with Razorpay or PhonePe would be helpful. Right now I have to check there and then come back here to mark it paid.

Custom clothing seller
05 Key findings and outcome
Order status needed one canonical location, not two

Users valued visibility but were frustrated by repetition. Seeing the same status update in both the chat thread and the right panel felt redundant, it created doubt about which one was authoritative.

Multi-order handling was broken for custom-product businesses

Users managing multiple orders from one customer had no way to track them separately. This wasn't an edge case, it was a daily workflow for the majority of participants. Unique order IDs were an unambiguous, unmet need.

Minimalism outperformed configurability in status design

A clean 3-stage system, Ordered, Paid, Shipped, was faster and less error-prone than the more configurable dropdown design. Participants didn't want power; they wanted clarity.

📍
What changed

Findings gave Meta's product team clear, participant-grounded evidence for redesign priorities. The unique order ID gap and payment automation need, both invisible in earlier assumptions, became validated requirements for the next design iteration.

Maruti Suzuki, EV Charging App & Infotainment Research

10-week mixed-method study to validate whether the connected EV ecosystem could support real-world charging before launch

MSILDiary StudyMixed MethodMobility
+
Client
Maruti Suzuki India Ltd.
Domain
Mobility / EV
Methods
Diary Study, Moderated UT (in-person), Contextual Interviews, Competitive Benchmarking
My role
Research planning, moderation, diary study facilitation, synthesis, stakeholder reporting
01 Research question
Can this EV charging app and infotainment ecosystem support a reliable real-world charging experience, or will it fail users at the moments that matter most?
02 Why this method

The research started with in-person moderated usability testing at actual MSIL charging stations. Early sessions quickly revealed a fundamental limitation: a controlled session captures whether someone can use the app, but not whether they trust it enough to stake a journey on it. Trust in a charging app isn't formed in a single interaction, it's built or broken over weeks of real use, failed sessions, and recovered anxieties. A lab environment couldn't surface that. So we evolved the approach. The next phase was a longitudinal diary study, where participants were given the app and asked to use it for their real charging needs over three weeks, logging sessions, emotions, and failures as they happened. This also let us observe whether users were following the intended task flows or developing workarounds, and where those workarounds appeared in the journey. I ran two participant batches deliberately, using what the in-person sessions taught us to sharpen the diary tasks and protocol before the second cohort.

03 How I worked
01
Hypothesis journey map

Mapped assumed user journey before fieldwork to align team on what we were testing

02
In-person UT (Batch 1)

Moderated sessions at MSIL charging stations, surfacing the limits of what a controlled setting could capture

03
Diary study (3 weeks)

50 participants used the app for real charging over 3 weeks, logging sessions, failures, and task approaches as they happened

04
Competitive benchmark

Evaluated Statiq, ChargeZone, Tata EZ Charge, and Zeon across 6 dimensions

05
Failure point synthesis

Task success matrices and affinity mapping across 5 core user journeys

🔎

How I synthesised: I built a task success matrix mapping every participant against every task, then looked for patterns in where failures clustered. The UPI charging initiation task failed at 73%, that number alone shifted product priorities. I also tracked the emotional arc from diary entries, looking for the specific moments where optimism turned to doubt and doubt turned to learned distrust. That emotional framing became central to how findings were presented to stakeholders.

⚠️

What we had to adapt: Batch 1 in-person sessions at dealerships ran into real fieldwork problems. Some participants were denied access to chargers, cars blocked EV bays, and a few sessions couldn't be completed as designed. Rather than push through with compromised data, we used those sessions to understand why the physical environment was creating friction, and fed that directly into the diary study design. Batch 2 protocols were also revised after Batch 1 revealed that UPI payment flows were getting auto-cancelled in ways we hadn't anticipated, so we introduced task workarounds and explicit timeout guidance for Batch 2 participants.

04 What participants said

It said the session was active. I was standing outside for 20 minutes. The car wasn't charging. I didn't know if I'd been charged or not.

EV owner, Delhi

I bookmarked it on my phone. Then I get in the car and none of my bookmarks are there. I have to search all over again while I'm driving.

Daily commuter, Bangalore
05 Key findings and outcome
Charging completion was the only measure of app success

Every other feature, trip planning, bookmarking, infotainment, was judged through one lens: did it reliably help me charge? One failure reset trust entirely, regardless of how many sessions had worked before.

UPI charging initiation failed at 73%, the most critical task in the journey

Payment flow failures, unexplained session rejections, and misleading "session active" states trapped users in retry loops with no guidance. The app looked calm while users were stranded outside.

The mobile app and infotainment system were not one product

Bookmarks, filters, and session history from the mobile app did not appear in infotainment. Users discovered this mid-journey, while driving, and had to rebuild their setup from scratch. The two systems felt disconnected because they were.

📍
What changed

Pre-launch research surfaced critical failure points across the charging initiation flow and infotainment integration. The 73% failure rate on Task 3 and the session state mismatch were escalated as launch blockers, findings that would not have been visible in a lab-based study.

In-Car Infotainment Systems, Generative Research

Exploratory research to understand how drivers actually use in-car systems and what the safety and design implications are

AutomotiveGenerative ResearchBehaviouralSafety
+
Domain
Automotive / Consumer
Type
Generative / Exploratory
Methods
In-depth Interviews, Secondary Research, Red Route Analysis, Behavioural Mapping
My role
Research design, moderation, secondary synthesis, insight generation
01 Research question
Which infotainment features do drivers actually use, which ones create distraction risk, and what do users genuinely want from the next generation of in-car interfaces?
02 Why this method

There was no prototype to evaluate, this was generative research to build product strategy from scratch. I started with secondary research because the domain had existing safety literature on driver distraction that would have taken primary research months to replicate. That literature review shaped exactly which assumptions I needed primary research to challenge. I chose in-depth interviews over surveys because I needed to understand the reasoning behind behaviour, why someone reaches for their phone instead of using voice command, not just that they do it. I applied a Red Route Analysis to separate what users say they want from what they actually use, which in this domain turned out to be quite different.

03 How I worked
01
Secondary research

Literature review of global safety studies, industry reports, and driver distraction research

02
9 in-depth interviews

Mid-range car owners across varying tech familiarity, covering 6 topic areas

03
Red Route Analysis

Mapped features used by everyone always, frequently, occasionally, or rarely

04
Failure pattern mapping

Documented 3 distinct distraction cascade patterns with safety implications for each

05
Strategy output

Opportunity areas for AI integration, interface simplification, and physical control retention

🔎

How I synthesised: The most revealing pattern wasn't what users did, it was the cascade: voice command fails, so they reach for the touchscreen; that's unclear, so they reach for the phone. Each step adds distraction. I mapped this as a failure chain rather than three separate problems, because the design solution had to interrupt the chain, not just fix one link. That framing changed the recommendation from "improve voice recognition" to "reduce dependency on any single input modality."

04 What participants said

I can do it on my phone faster than on the screen. And I know it's wrong. But the system just doesn't recognise what I'm saying half the time.

Mid-range car owner

I prefer buttons for AC and volume. I don't want to look for it on a screen. My hand just knew where the knob was.

Daily commuter
05 Key findings and outcome
The infotainment system is treated as a phone extension, not a standalone interface

CarPlay and Android Auto dominated actual daily usage. The inbuilt system was a fallback. Design strategy that assumed users would engage with the inbuilt system was built on a false premise.

Touchscreen-only routine controls introduce a documented safety risk

Moving AC and volume to touch UI requires visual attention for a task that previously used zero. Secondary research corroborated this with 19 to 26 second distraction windows per touchscreen interaction, data that reframed the design problem as a safety problem.

60%+ of features were unknown to users, an IA failure, not a feature failure

Users weren't ignoring features. They had no reason to explore menus while driving. Features buried in navigation hierarchies simply didn't exist for most users in practice.

📍
What changed

Research reframed the design problem: infotainment is not a standalone product competing for attention, it's a supporting layer for a phone-first experience. That shift redirected the product strategy from adding features to simplifying access, and elevated physical control retention as a safety requirement rather than a legacy preference.

02

How I think

01

Method follows question, not convenience

I don't have a default method. I have a question, and I choose the method that gives the most honest answer to that question given the constraints of time, access, and what the team actually needs to decide. A diary study and a usability test can both be wrong for the same project, depending on what you're actually trying to learn.

02

Workarounds are the most honest data

When someone builds a spreadsheet to do what the product should do, or calls a colleague instead of checking the system, I treat that as the most important signal in the session. It doesn't automatically mean the design failed — it might mean the workflow was never supported, or trust in the data broke down, or training never happened. The workaround tells me where to look. The research tells me why.

03

Frequency is not the same as importance

The finding that appeared in seven out of seven sessions isn't always the most important one. Sometimes one participant surfaces a single assumption the entire product is built on, and that matters more than consensus. I try to separate what's common from what's consequential.

04

Research that doesn't change a decision has zero impact

A report that confirms what everyone already believed, sits in a folder, or gets read once and set aside has failed regardless of how rigorous the method was. I measure the success of research by whether the team made a different decision because of it, not by how well the presentation went.

05

Design background is a research advantage

My degree is in product design. That means I speak the language of the teams I work with, I understand what a finding needs to look like to actually influence a design decision, and I can participate in conversations about tradeoffs rather than just handing over a report and leaving.

06

Good synthesis means choosing what not to say

The hardest part of synthesis is cutting. Every session produces observations, but not every observation earns a place in the findings. Deciding what to leave out, what to hold back, and what the team genuinely needs to act is where research either gets sharp or gets buried in noise.

03

How I work

Research is only as good as the decisions it shapes. My process is designed around one principle: every step should either sharpen the question or sharpen the answer. Nothing else earns its place.

01

Scope & Frame

Before writing a single screener question, I work with stakeholders to agree on what a successful research outcome looks like. What decision will this research inform? What would we need to learn to make it confidently? What assumptions are we most afraid to be wrong about?

This step often surfaces that the team has multiple research questions pointing in different directions. Resolving that before fieldwork saves weeks of misaligned synthesis later.

Outputs
Research brief Stakeholder alignment session Prioritised research questions
02

Design & Recruit

Method choice is a research decision, not a default. I select methods based on the type of question, not habit: a behavioural problem that unfolds over time needs a diary study; a design decision that affects task flow needs moderated usability testing; an exploratory question about why users behave a certain way needs in-depth interviews with space to follow unexpected threads.

Recruitment is where most research goes wrong quietly. I write screeners that filter for people who have actually experienced the problem, not people who are available and willing. Participant quality determines insight quality.

Outputs
Research plan Discussion guide Screener with qualifying logic Recruitment brief
03

Field & Capture

I moderate with a structured guide and an open mind. The guide keeps sessions comparable across participants. The open mind catches what the guide didn't anticipate, which is often the most valuable thing in the data. When a participant says something unexpected, I follow it, because that's usually where the real insight lives.

I document in layers: task completion and errors during sessions, detailed notes immediately after, and emerging hypotheses in a running debrief document. This discipline means synthesis starts in the field, not after it.

Outputs
Session recordings and notes Daily debriefs Emerging pattern log
04

Synthesise

Synthesis is where the real intellectual work happens, and where most research loses its value if done carelessly. I separate observation from interpretation rigorously: "the user clicked three times before finding the button" is an observation; "the navigation hierarchy doesn't match the user's mental model" is an interpretation. Both matter, but conflating them early closes off better explanations.

I use affinity mapping to cluster observations, then look for patterns that cut across participants, not just themes that appear often. Frequency matters less than whether a finding changes what the team should do. A single participant quote that exposes a fundamental product assumption can outweigh a consensus finding that confirms what everyone already knew.

Outputs
Affinity map Insight clusters Journey map Opportunity areas
05

Activate

A research report that sits in a folder has zero impact. Activation means getting findings into the room where decisions are made, in a form that makes acting on them easier than ignoring them. That means different things for different stakeholders: a two-page topline for a product lead making a sprint decision; a synthesis workshop for a design team aligning on a problem space; an opportunity map for leadership deciding what to build next.

I measure the success of research not by how well the report is received, but by whether the team made a different decision because of it.

Outputs
Topline report Synthesis workshop Stakeholder presentation Prioritised recommendations
